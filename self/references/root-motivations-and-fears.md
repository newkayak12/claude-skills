# Root Motivations and Core Fears — Shared Reference

This file is a shared reference for `motivation-explorer` and `fear-inventory`. It provides taxonomies of root motivations and core fears, maps how they mirror each other, and offers guidance on distinguishing surface from root level.

---

## Root Motivations Taxonomy

Beneath most surface motivations, a small set of root drivers recur across people and contexts. These are not personality types — they are active needs whose fulfillment (or chronic frustration) shapes behavior.

| Root Motivation | Core Drive | Common Surface Forms |
|----------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Autonomy** | To direct one's own life; to not be controlled or obligated | Resistance to authority, need for creative freedom, difficulty with constraint |
| **Mastery** | To grow in competence; to meet challenge; to get better at things that matter | Perfectionism, deep work, frustration at stagnation, flow-seeking |
| **Belonging** | To be accepted, included, and genuinely known by others | People-pleasing, conformity, over-investing in relationships, fear of exclusion |
| **Significance** | To matter; to be seen, acknowledged, and respected | Status-seeking, recognition-driven effort, difficulty tolerating being overlooked |
| **Security** | To be protected from loss, instability, and uncertainty | Risk-aversion, resource accumulation, control behaviors, difficulty with change |
| **Contribution** | To give to something larger than oneself; to leave things better | Meaning-seeking, purpose-driven work, volunteer orientation, frustration at pointless effort |
| **Worthiness** | To believe one is enough — capable, lovable, deserving | Achievement-driven identity, perfectionism, chronic self-doubt, imposter syndrome |
| **Connection** | To be in genuine relationship; to love and be loved | Intimacy-seeking, loneliness sensitivity, strong emotional attunement |
| **Freedom** | To have options, not be trapped; to be able to leave or change | Non-commitment patterns, keeping escape routes open, high sensitivity to obligation |
| **Growth** | To keep becoming; to not stagnate or repeat | Restlessness in comfort, constant learning, aversion to routine |

Most people have two to three dominant root motivations that operate beneath many different surface goals. A single behavior (e.g., working extremely hard) can be driven by very different roots (worthiness, security, mastery, or recognition) — and the root determines what would actually satisfy it.

## Core Fears Taxonomy

Core fears are the fundamental threats underneath surface worries. They are not situational — they recur across different domains of life and often run beneath many surface fears simultaneously.

| Core Fear | What It Threatens | Common Surface Forms |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Inadequacy** | I am not enough — not smart, capable, worthy, or lovable enough | Fear of failure, imposter syndrome, avoiding challenges, perfectionism as self-protection |
| **Rejection** | I will be found unacceptable and cast out by those I need | People-pleasing, hiding authentic preferences, avoiding conflict, over-monitoring others' reactions |
| **Abandonment** | Those I depend on will leave; I will be fundamentally alone | Clinginess, jealousy, preemptive self-sufficiency, difficulty trusting |
| **Loss of control** | Things will spin out of control and I will be helpless | Over-planning, micromanagement, difficulty delegating, anxiety when plans change |
| **Meaninglessness** | My life, choices, and effort will not matter | Existential restlessness, difficulty with "pointless" tasks, urgency about legacy |
| **Humiliation** | I will be exposed and publicly shamed | Avoiding visibility, hiding vulnerability, extreme sensitivity to criticism |
| **Entrapment** | I will be stuck, cornered, with no way out | Difficulty committing, keeping options open, claustrophobic relationships |
| **Death/Nonexistence** | I will cease to exist — or those I love will | Health anxiety, legacy preoccupation, difficulty with mortality reminders |
| **Failure** | My actions will produce bad outcomes I cannot reverse | Paralysis, excessive deliberation, avoidance of any situation without guaranteed success |
| **Exposure** | What I hide will be discovered and I will be judged | Inauthenticity, exhausting performance, double life between public and private self |

## How Motivations and Fears Mirror Each Other

Root motivations and core fears are often the same psychological need viewed from opposite directions. The motivation is the pull toward what is wanted; the fear is the dread of its absence or opposite.

| Root Motivation | Mirrors | Core Fear |
|----------------|---------|-----------|
| Belonging | ↔ | Rejection / Abandonment |
| Worthiness | ↔ | Inadequacy / Humiliation |
| Security | ↔ | Loss of control / Failure |
| Significance | ↔ | Meaninglessness / Being overlooked |
| Autonomy / Freedom | ↔ | Entrapment |
| Connection | ↔ | Abandonment / Isolation |
| Mastery / Growth | ↔ | Inadequacy / Stagnation |
| Contribution | ↔ | Meaninglessness |

This mirroring has a practical implication: the same root can be approached from either direction in a conversation. Understanding the motivation reveals what the person is reaching toward. Understanding the fear reveals what they are trying to avoid. Both are valid entry points — and both point to the same underlying need.

## Distinguishing Surface from Root Level

Most people present their fears and motivations at the surface level. The surface level is real — it is what is consciously experienced. But it is rarely the full story.

### Signs You Are Still at the Surface

- The motivation or fear could apply to almost anyone in this situation
- Asking "why does that matter?" or "what would that mean?" produces a deeper answer readily
- The person has solved for this motivation/fear before without the underlying issue resolving
- The same pattern shows up across very different life domains (work, relationships, health) — suggesting a common root

### Moving from Surface to Root

**For motivations:** Use the peeling protocol — ask "why does that matter to me?" or "what would having that give me?" 3-5 times. When answers start to feel fundamental (touching identity, safety, love, meaning), you are near the root.

**For fears:** Ask "what would be true about me or my life if this fear came to pass?" Layer downward: surface event → personal implication → identity or existential threat. When the answer is one of the core fears above, you have found the root.

### Key Diagnostic Question

The most reliable test: *If this motivation were fully satisfied / if this fear never came to pass — would the person feel genuinely at peace, or would they quickly find another version of the same concern?*

If the answer is "they'd quickly find another version" — the root has not yet been named. The surface form changes; the root stays constant until it is seen clearly.
