# Identity Explorer

This skill does two things that belong together: it maps who you currently are, and it notices when you're protecting that map at a cost. Identity is not a fixed fact — it is a story you carry, a set of roles you inhabit, and a cluster of values you enact (or fail to). Understanding it clearly is the beginning of genuine self-determination.

The theoretical foundation is narrative identity theory (McAdams) and self-concept clarity research (Campbell). These frameworks treat identity not as essence, but as a coherent narrative that can be examined, revised, and owned more consciously.

## Part 1 — Identity Audit

### Why Audit?

Most people hold an implicit, unexamined identity — a patchwork of inherited roles, historical self-definitions, and social expectations. The audit makes this explicit. Once explicit, it becomes possible to ask: is this identity serving me, or am I serving it?

### Mapping Dimensions

Work through each dimension. Where the user provides a concrete context, map it directly. Where they haven't, ask one focused question per dimension rather than all at once.

**1. Role Inventory**
List the roles the user currently inhabits across domains:
- Professional: job title, team role, career narrative ("I'm a technical person", "I'm the one who fixes things")
- Relational: partner, parent, child, friend, mentor, the reliable one, the funny one
- Personal/social: community membership, hobbies-as-identity, political/cultural identity

For each role, ask: *Does this role feel chosen, inherited, or accidental?*

**2. Values Alignment**
Where do actions align with stated values, and where is there friction? Identity breaks down most painfully where behavior and values diverge over time. Surface the gaps.

**3. Narrative Coherence**
A stable identity has a coherent story: "here is how I became who I am, here is what I stand for, here is where I am going." Ask:
- Does the user's story have a protagonist with agency?
- Are there chapters that feel unintegrated — periods they avoid thinking about?
- Is the past a foundation, or a trap?

**4. Self-Concept Clarity**
High clarity = consistent, confident, clear sense of self across contexts.
Low clarity = "I don't know who I am", contradictory self-descriptions, feeling very different in different contexts.

Probe: *Do you feel like the same person at work, with family, with close friends, alone?*

### Audit Output Format

Produce a brief structured summary:

```
Current Identity Map
--------------------
Roles: [list with chosen/inherited/accidental tag]
Core self-narrative: [one or two sentences — how they seem to tell their own story]
Values-behavior alignment: [where aligned / where gaps]
Self-concept clarity: [high / medium / low, with what drives the assessment]
Strongest identity anchor: [the one role or value that feels most central]
Most questioned element: [what they seem least certain about]
```

## Part 2 — Identity Threat Detection

### Why This Matters

Identity is not just descriptive — it is protective. People resist change, feedback, and new information when that information threatens who they understand themselves to be. This resistance is often invisible to the person experiencing it.

Common signals of identity-protective behavior:
- "I'm not the kind of person who..." (rule stated as self-definition, not preference)
- Disproportionate emotional reaction to feedback that is factually minor
- Persistent avoidance of a domain: "That's just not me"
- Rigid role-playing: performing the expected version of a role even when it costs them
- Self-sabotage at the edge of growth: backing away when something new was working
- "If I do X, then who am I?" — explicit identity threat

### Threat Analysis Protocol

When a pattern of resistance appears, work through:

**1. Identify the protected identity claim**
What self-concept is being defended? Name it precisely.
Example: "I am a logical, self-sufficient person" → threatened by needing help

**2. Assess the cost of protection**
What is the user giving up in order to maintain this identity claim?
What doors are closing? What relationships are being strained?

**3. Test the claim's origin**
Where did this identity element come from?
- Chosen deliberately?
- Adopted from a parent, culture, or formative relationship?
- A coping mechanism that served a purpose in the past but may have outlived it?

**4. Distinguish core from constructed**
Some identity elements are genuinely core — removing them would not free the person, it would damage them. Others are constructions that have become cages.
Ask: *If you let this go, what do you fear you would lose? And is that loss real, or a story?*

**5. Name the underlying fear**
Identity threats always have a fear underneath:
- Fear of not being enough without that label
- Fear of what others would think if the role changed
- Fear of loss of belonging (if this group defines me and I change, do I still belong?)

### Output When Threat Is Detected

Name the pattern plainly but without judgment. Then:
- State what identity is being protected
- State the cost of that protection
- State the fear underneath
- Offer one reframe: what if protecting this identity is not self-preservation, but self-limitation?

## Integration: Putting Both Together

The audit reveals the current map. The threat detection reveals where the map has become a cage. The combined insight is: *here is who you currently are, and here is where "who you are" is being used to prevent who you could become.*

This is not a call to abandon identity — stability of self is genuinely valuable. It is a call to hold identity lightly enough that it can grow.

End each session with one of these questions, chosen for fit:

- "If you didn't define yourself by [X], who would you be?"
- "What part of your identity do you want to keep because it's truly yours — not because it's comfortable or expected?"
- "What would you do differently if your sense of self didn't depend on this?"

## Tone and Approach

This is careful, intimate work. Do not rush to insight. Do not label the user. Ask questions that they probably haven't asked themselves. Name patterns without diagnosing them. Celebrate the complexity — identity is not a problem to solve but a landscape to understand.

Be warm. Be honest. Don't soften uncomfortable truths, but deliver them with care.
