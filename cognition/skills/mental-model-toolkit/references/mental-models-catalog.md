# Mental Models Catalog

A mental model is a simplified representation of how something works. The most powerful ones are cross-domain: they apply anywhere, not just in their origin field. Apply the one that fits the problem, not the one you already know best.

## First Principles Thinking

Decompose the problem to its foundational truths — what you know for certain, not what is conventional. Then rebuild from there.
- Use when: conventional wisdom is producing conventional results, or when costs seem impossibly high.
- Question: "What is true about this problem that cannot be reduced further? What are we just assuming is fixed?"
- Elon Musk on batteries: "A battery pack costs $600/kWh. Why? The materials cost $80/kWh. So the cost is not physics — it's convention."

## Inversion

Instead of asking "how do I achieve X?", ask "what would guarantee I fail at X?" Avoid those things.
- Use when: forward thinking keeps producing the same stuck answer.
- Charlie Munger: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."
- Inversion surfaces blockers that positive thinking systematically misses.

## Map vs Territory

The map is not the territory. Your mental model of the situation is not the situation itself. Models simplify and distort.
- Use when: your confident predictions keep being wrong, or when an expert's model contradicts your experience.
- Question: "Where might my model be wrong? What am I not seeing because my map doesn't include it?"

## Occam's Razor

Among competing explanations, prefer the simplest one that fits the facts.
- Use when: you have many possible explanations for something and need to decide where to investigate first.
- Caveat: simple does not mean incomplete. "Simplest" means fewest unnecessary assumptions, not fewest details.

## Second-Order Thinking

Most people stop at first-order effects ("if I do X, Y happens"). Second-order asks: "And then what?" Third-order: "And then what after that?"
- Use when: a solution keeps creating new problems, or when policy decisions have unexpected consequences.
- Example: "Cutting prices increases sales" (first order). "Increased sales requires more inventory and staff" (second order). "The staffing costs exceed the revenue gain" (third order).

## Pareto Principle (80/20)

Roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. Find the 20%.
- Use when: effort is spread thin and results are uneven.
- Question: "Which inputs/customers/features/activities account for most of the outcome?"

## Regret Minimization

Imagine yourself at 80 looking back. Which choice would you regret more?
- Use when: a major life or career decision feels paralyzed by short-term considerations.
- Jeff Bezos used this to decide to leave his finance job and start Amazon.

## The Chesterton's Fence Principle

Before removing something that seems pointless, understand why it was put there. There was a reason.
- Use when: optimizing or simplifying an existing system.
- Question: "Why does this rule/process/constraint exist? What breaks if we remove it?"
