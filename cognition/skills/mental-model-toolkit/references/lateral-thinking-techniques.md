# Lateral Thinking Techniques

Lateral thinking (Edward de Bono) generates solutions that are not reachable by straightforward logical extension of the current frame. It breaks the "gravity" of the obvious.

The obvious solution is obvious because it is the path of least resistance in the current framing. Lateral thinking changes the frame.

## Technique 1: Random Entry

Introduce a random, unrelated concept and force a connection to the problem.

Exercise: pick a random word (animal, object, verb). Ask: "How is [random word] related to this problem? What does [random word] suggest about a possible solution?"

Example: Problem is "slow customer support." Random word: "garden." Connections: tending, patience, growth, seasons, pruning. Insight: "What if we proactively tended to customers before problems grew, rather than waiting for them to contact us?" → Leads to proactive check-in model.

The connection does not have to be logical. The randomness forces new pathways.

## Technique 2: Provocation (Po)

State a deliberately absurd provocation and use it as a stepping stone. De Bono's notation: "Po" (provocation):

- "Po: cars have square wheels." Exploring this: square wheels create bumps at regular intervals → sensors at regular intervals → suspension that adapts predictively to road patterns.
- "Po: customers pay us to have bad experiences." Exploring: they pay to test our recovery → we build the best recovery process in the industry → recovery becomes a competitive advantage.

The provocation is not a suggestion. It is a direction-finder.

## Technique 3: Reversal

Reverse the goal and see what you find:

- Instead of "how do we acquire more users?", ask "how do we make it easy for users to leave?" (reveals: what keeps them, what friction points exist, what they value)
- Instead of "how do we speed up this process?", ask "how do we make it maximally slow?" (reveals: where the drag actually comes from)

## Technique 4: Analogical Thinking

Find a domain that has already solved a structurally similar problem. Borrow the solution.

- "How do airlines handle the problem of [X]?" "How does a hospital handle [X]?" "How does evolution handle [X]?"
- The constraint: the analogy must be structural, not superficial. "Both involve people" is not structural. "Both involve coordinating many independent agents with partial information" is structural.

## Technique 5: Constraint Removal

Ask: "What would we do if [constraint] did not exist?" Generate the ideal solution without the constraint, then work backward: how much of that ideal can we achieve even with the constraint? What is the minimum version of the constraint-free solution that still captures most of the value?
