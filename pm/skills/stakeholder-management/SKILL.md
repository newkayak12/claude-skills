# Stakeholder Management

Getting the right people to say yes — and keeping them saying yes throughout execution.

## Core Principle

Stakeholder management is not politics. It is information architecture. Stakeholders resist or disengage because they feel uninformed, unheard, or threatened. Your job is to remove those conditions systematically — before they surface as blockers.

---

## Step 1: Build the Stakeholder Map

Create a 2×2 grid: **Influence** (high/low) on the Y-axis, **Interest** (high/low) on the X-axis.

| Quadrant | Type | Strategy |
|----------|------|----------|
| High influence, high interest | **Key Players** | Engage deeply, frequent 1:1s, co-create decisions |
| High influence, low interest | **Keep Satisfied** | Brief updates, ask before big moves, no surprises |
| Low influence, high interest | **Keep Informed** | Regular updates, create channel for input |
| Low influence, low interest | **Monitor** | Minimal contact; escalate only if status changes |

**For each Key Player, answer:**
- What is their primary goal this quarter?
- What would make this initiative a win for them personally?
- What is their biggest concern or risk?
- Who do they trust? (influences your messenger choice)
- What's their preferred communication style? (data-heavy, narrative, verbal)?

---

## Step 2: Set Communication Cadence

Match cadence to quadrant — over-communicating to the wrong people wastes capital.

| Stakeholder Type | Cadence | Format |
|-----------------|---------|--------|
| Executive sponsor | Weekly async + bi-weekly sync | 1-page status memo or Slack summary |
| Cross-functional leads | Bi-weekly sync | Agenda-driven meeting, decision log shared after |
| Adjacent team leads | Monthly | Brief email with asks/FYIs clearly separated |
| Broad org (low interest) | At milestones only | Company-wide update, all-hands mention |

**One-page status memo structure:**
1. What we decided / shipped since last update (2 sentences)
2. What we're working on now (2 sentences)
3. What we need from you (explicit ask — never bury this)
4. What's at risk (one honest sentence)

---

## Step 3: Diagnose Resistance

Before you try to persuade, identify which type of resistance you're facing.

| Resistance Pattern | Root Cause | Approach |
|-------------------|-----------|---------|
| "This conflicts with my team's priorities" | Resource competition | Reframe as shared outcome; find the overlap |
| "I don't trust the data" | Credibility gap | Show methodology; invite them to poke holes early |
| "We tried this before and it failed" | Historical precedent | Acknowledge; explain what's different now |
| "I wasn't involved in this decision" | Exclusion / loss of control | Give meaningful input opportunity; even symbolic works |
| "This will hurt my metrics" | Personal risk | Find guardrails; offer to own the risk jointly |
| Silent non-response | Power play or disengagement | Name it directly in 1:1: "I'm not getting a clear signal — what's the concern?" |

---

## Step 4: Build Buy-In Progressively

Work the room before the room.

1. **Pre-wire decisions:** Talk to every Key Player 1:1 before any group meeting. Your goal: no surprises in the room. If someone will object, know it before the meeting — and address it before the meeting.

2. **Find allies first:** Start with the stakeholder most likely to agree. Their visible support shifts the social dynamic for neutral stakeholders.

3. **Use the "WIIFM" frame:** Every 1:1 should start with their goal, not yours. Lead with what the initiative does for them, then ask for what you need.

4. **Make their concerns visible:** In group settings, proactively name objections before others raise them. "I know some of us are concerned about X — here's how we're thinking about it." This defuses the adversarial dynamic.

5. **Document decisions with stakeholder names:** "At the Dec 4 review, [Name] and [Name] agreed to proceed with Option B." This prevents revisiting.

---

## Step 5: How to Say No Without Burning Bridges

Saying no is necessary. How you do it determines whether the relationship survives.

**Formula:** Acknowledge → Explain the trade-off → Offer an alternative → Keep the door open

Example:
> "I hear you — faster checkout would be a real win for conversion. Right now we're holding firm on shipping the retention flow first because our data shows churn is costing us 3x more per month than conversion gains could offset. What I can do is put checkout in the next planning cycle and flag it as the first candidate when capacity frees up. Does that work?"

**Patterns to use:**
- "Yes, and later" — agree on the value, commit to a future slot
- "Yes, if" — agree conditional on resources, trade-off, or scope change
- "Not this, but this instead" — redirect to an alternative that partially meets the need
- "Let me show you the trade-off" — present the cost of saying yes to them (what comes out)

**Never:** Silently deprioritize without explanation. Nothing burns bridges faster than a stakeholder discovering their request was ignored.

---

## Step 6: Conflict Resolution Patterns

When two stakeholders want incompatible things:

**Pattern 1 — Common goal reframe**
Identify the shared upstream goal. "We both want revenue to grow. The question is whether we get there faster through acquisition or retention. Here's the data..."

**Pattern 2 — Time-box the disagreement**
"Let's run the retention experiment for 6 weeks. If retention KR doesn't move by 10%, we revisit." Converts a binary conflict into a testable bet.

**Pattern 3 — Explicit trade-off documentation**
Write down both options and their consequences. Ask each stakeholder to sign off on their preferred option knowing the cost. Accountability changes conversations.

**Pattern 4 — Escalation with a recommendation**
When you've genuinely tried to resolve it: "I've tried to find common ground between [A] and [B]. I recommend [X] because [reason]. I'd like you to make the call." Escalating without a recommendation is abdication.

---

## Escalation Path

Use escalation as a last resort, not a shortcut.

1. Attempt bilateral resolution (1:1 with each party)
2. Facilitated conversation with both parties present
3. Bring in a neutral third party (another PM lead, your manager)
4. Formal escalation to shared manager — with your recommendation

When escalating: bring the decision framed as a trade-off, not a complaint. "Here are the two options, their trade-offs, and my recommendation. We need a call by [date]."

## Quick Diagnostic

| Signal | What It Means | Action |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| A VP keeps postponing your meeting | Low interest or hidden concern | Ask directly: "Is there a concern I should know about?" |
| A stakeholder re-raises a closed decision | Didn't feel heard when it was made | Re-open briefly; let them voice it; reclose with documentation |
| Passive agreement in meetings, no action after | Not genuinely bought in | Identify the real concern; use resistance diagnosis |
| Conflicting directives from two execs | Misalignment above you | Surface it explicitly; request a joint call to align |
| No response to your updates | Wrong format, wrong frequency, or wrong level of detail | Ask what they actually want to see |
